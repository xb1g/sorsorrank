# Product

## Register

product

## Users

SorsorRank is for young, online Thai users who follow public affairs through social feeds; friends who want a low-effort civic conversation starter; civic-curious users who do not want to read long candidate or public-figure pages first; and, later, researchers or journalists interested in aggregate curiosity trends.

Users arrive in a fast, mobile, group-chat context. They should be able to understand the premise quickly, give explicit consent before any stored interaction, complete 10 sparse cards, jump to Google for their own research, and share that they participated without implying support, opposition, vote intent, or endorsement.

## Product Purpose

SorsorRank is a daily civic research swipe app: users do 10 public-figure cards per day, choose `Research` or `Skip`, and contribute to aggregate Research Interest Rank. The product exists to make civic curiosity feel lightweight, social, and repeatable while pushing users outward to search for themselves.

Success means users complete the daily 10, share neutral completion cards, invite friends into the same ritual, return on following days, and understand that rankings are aggregate curiosity signals only. The product must not become voting advice, an approval rating, an election prediction, a candidate battle, a comment forum, or a hosted source of political claims.

## Brand Personality

Fast, curious, restrained.

The interface should feel like a clean civic game: playful enough to complete and share, calm enough for public-figure and election-sensitive contexts, and sparse enough that the app never looks like it is making claims about people. The voice is direct, neutral, and challenge-oriented: "Do your 10. Search for yourself. See what people are researching."

## Anti-references

SorsorRank must not look or sound like a dating app clone, campaign poster, prediction market, approval poll, debate feed, political identity quiz, or candidate-comparison battle. Avoid hearts, flames, red/green moral judgment colors, romantic or sexual metaphors, attack framing, loud campaign energy, dark-pattern streak pressure, and any UI that makes public figures feel like entertainment targets.

Do not use public UI, CTA, share, rank, or growth copy that frames the product around support, endorsement, winning, approval, odds, predictions, heat, matches, or crushes. Do not host biographies, allegations, accusations, search snippets, editorial descriptions, negative labels, user-submitted political text, comments, quote posts, AI summaries, or monarchy, royal-family, or royal-institution content.

## Design Principles

1. Curiosity over persuasion. Every mechanic should invite independent research, not steer users toward a political conclusion.
2. Participation is shareable, preferences are not. Viral surfaces should show that someone completed the daily ritual, never who they researched, skipped, liked, opposed, or supported.
3. Sparse cards, clear actions. A card can show a name, optional role or party for disambiguation, a neutral Google Search button, and Research or Skip actions. Anything more raises product and legal risk.
4. Aggregate-first by default. Rankings, share snapshots, and metrics must be thresholded, consented, and framed as aggregate research interest, not individual opinion or public support.
5. Safety is product scope. Consent, privacy notice, freeze mode, methodology, roster audit, takedown/contact paths, short retention, and legal-review gates are core UX requirements, not back-office polish.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.2 AA for public surfaces. The app must work on small mobile screens, support readable Thai and English names, preserve large tap targets for thumb use, and provide tap-button fallbacks for any swipe gesture. Long public-figure names must not break card layout.

Respect reduced-motion preferences. Motion can be tactile and playful, but major card movement and completion effects must have reduced alternatives. Do not rely on color alone to communicate state, especially because red/green judgment colors are also off-brand. Every user-facing screen needs loading, empty, error, success, offline or slow-network, long-name, and small-mobile states.

Consent and privacy language must be plain and readable. Users who decline consent should still be able to read methodology, privacy, and contact pages without submitting swipes.
